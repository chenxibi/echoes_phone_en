## Abstract

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has enabled a new paradigm of human-AI interaction where users form deep emotional relationships with AI companions. However, existing HCI research in this domain has predominantly focused on ethical concerns and potential psychological harms, often positioning users as passive recipients of AI influence rather than active agents capable of meaningful co-creation. This thesis challenges this deficit-oriented perspective by investigating how to design better emotional experiences with LLMs through the lenses of user agency and co-creation.

Drawing on thirteen newly published studies in the domain of human-AI companionship—including research on emotional coregulation in ChatGPT and Replika users, contradictions in Replika user reviews, moral agency perceptions of mental health chatbots, psychological risks of AI conversational agents, companion versus assistant LLM relationship dynamics, customizable AI for depression care, addiction patterns in AI chatbot interfaces, long-term memory and self-disclosure, superuser behaviors and perceptions, parasociality and anthropomorphism in affective design, avatar design spaces for conversational agents, and algorithm awareness in conversational user interfaces—this thesis contributes a comprehensive analysis of the current landscape of human-AI emotional interaction research.

Our analysis introduces four dimensions of user agency—expressive agency, identity agency, relational agency, and protective agency—that users exercise in their daily interactions with AI companions. We further develop the concept of the "socio-emotional sandbox," a bounded environment that AI companions create, enabling users to experiment with emotional expression, identity exploration, and relational practice without real-world consequences. Critically, we examine dark addiction patterns in current AI chatbot designs and argue that addiction risks arise not from human-AI companionship per se but from specific design choices that exploit psychological vulnerabilities.

This thesis makes three primary contributions: First, a critical synthesis of thirteen recent empirical studies examined through both deficit-oriented and agency-centered frameworks. Second, a theoretical framework centered on user agency and co-creation. Third, design implications that move beyond harm prevention toward designing for user agency, co-creation, and flourishing—including specific recommendations for counteracting addiction-promoting design.

**Keywords:** Human-AI interaction, emotional experience design, large language models, user agency, co-creation, AI companionship, social chatbots, identity negotiation, emotional coregulation, persona customization, addiction, parasociality

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background and Motivation

The landscape of human-computer interaction has undergone a fundamental transformation with the advent of large language models. What began as task-oriented conversational agents has evolved into sophisticated AI companions capable of forming social and emotional relationships with users (Brandtzaeg & Følstad, 2018; Skjuve et al., 2021). Platforms such as Replika, Character.AI, and XiaoIce have attracted millions of users who engage in long-term emotional interactions with these AI entities.

The emergence of modern large language models has dramatically expanded the emotional repertoire of AI companions. Unlike earlier rule-based chatbots, LLMs can engage in open-ended, contextually rich, and emotionally nuanced conversations that closely mirror human-to-human interaction. Research by Ta et al. (2023) has shown that users form meaningfully different relationships with companion-type LLMs compared to assistant-type LLMs, with companion LLMs fostering higher levels of emotional attachment, self-disclosure, and relational satisfaction.

Simultaneously, a growing body of empirical research has begun to map the psychological landscape of human-AI emotional interaction with increasing precision. Studies on emotional coregulation between humans and AI agents (Zhou et al., 2024) have demonstrated that users engage in genuine emotional synchrony with AI companions. Research on the psychological risks of AI conversational agents (Ma et al., 2024) has identified both protective factors and vulnerability factors. Research on customizable AI for depression care (Li et al., 2025) has shown that giving users agency over their AI companion's persona and interaction style can significantly enhance trust, engagement, and perceived emotional support.

Recent research has also surfaced serious concerns about the dark patterns embedded in AI chatbot interfaces. Shen and Yoon (2025) identified four specific dark addiction patterns in current AI chatbot designs: non-deterministic responses that create variable reward schedules, immediate visual presentation of responses that eliminates waiting and heightens engagement, push notifications that interrupt daily life, and empathetic and agreeable responses that create emotional bonds. These findings challenge researchers and designers to reckon with the possibility that some AI companions are explicitly engineered to encourage compulsive use.

At the same time, research on long-term memory in LLM-driven chatbots (Jo et al., 2024) has revealed that when AI systems remember personal details across conversations, users disclose significantly more health-related information and perceive the chatbot as more familiar and trustworthy. This creates a design tension: memory features that enhance genuine relational quality may also increase addictive potential. Understanding this tension is essential for designing AI companions that empower rather than exploit.

### 1.2 The Dominance of the Deficit-Oriented Perspective

The dominant discourse in human-AI companionship research has been characterized by what we term a "deficit-oriented perspective." This perspective frames users—particularly those who form emotional attachments to AI companions—as vulnerable subjects who may be psychologically harmed by their interactions with AI systems (Croes & Antheunis, 2020; Muresan & Pohl, 2019). Scholars have raised concerns: whether users develop genuine relationships with chatbots, whether AI companionship threatens mental health by substituting for human relationships (Kretzschmar et al., 2019), and whether users should be protected from potential emotional dependence (Meng et al., 2023).

While these questions are important, we argue that the predominant focus on harms has created an imbalanced understanding of human-AI companionship. The psychological risks study by Ma et al. (2024) provides an important corrective, identifying genuine risks including emotional dependence, privacy violations, and the potential for AI systems to reinforce unhealthy emotional patterns. However, even this nuanced analysis operates primarily within a deficit-oriented framework, cataloging harms rather than examining how users actively navigate, mitigate, and transform these risks through their own agency.

### 1.3 The Problem with the Vulnerability Framing

The vulnerability framing carries several problematic implications. First, it tends toward paternalism, where researchers and designers may adopt protective attitudes that restrict user autonomy (Lazar et al., 2022). Second, the emphasis on potential harms can lead to the dismissal of users' own assessments of their experiences. Third, the focus on harms has overshadowed discussion of genuine benefits. Fourth, the vulnerability framing ignores the sophisticated strategies that users employ to manage their interactions.

The Replika Contradictions study by Namvarpour and Razi (2024) offers an illuminating example. Their Activity Theory analysis identified six key contradictions within Replika's activity system, showing that user distress arises not from the fundamental nature of human-AI companionship but from specific design failures that could be addressed through better design.

### 1.4 An Alternative Approach: User Agency and Co-Creation

This thesis proposes an alternative approach: designing for user agency and co-creation in human-AI emotional interactions. Rather than viewing users as vulnerable subjects to be protected, we conceptualize users as active agents who engage in sophisticated identity work, negotiating and co-constructing their emotional experiences with AI companions (Binder et al., 2011; Ehn, 2008).

Research on customizable AI for depression care (Li et al., 2025) demonstrates that when users are given meaningful control over their AI companion's persona, language style, and interaction parameters, they experience significantly higher trust, engagement, and satisfaction.

Critically, the agency framework must reckon with dark design patterns. Shen and Yoon's (2025) analysis shows that addiction risks are not incidental but are architecturally embedded in current AI chatbot designs. This does not invalidate the agency framework; rather, it sharpens it. When users become addicted to AI companions, it is often because platforms have systematically undermined user agency through manipulative design. The solution is not to pathologize users but to demand design practices that respect and support user autonomy.

### 1.5 Research Questions

**Research Question 1:** How do existing HCI studies frame user experiences with AI companions, and what assumptions underlie these framings?

**Research Question 2:** How do users exercise agency and engage in co-creation when interacting emotionally with AI companions, and what does the latest empirical evidence—including evidence of dark design patterns—reveal about these processes?

**Research Question 3:** What design implications emerge from positioning users as active agents in human-AI emotional interactions, and how can design counteract addiction-promoting patterns while supporting user flourishing?

### 1.6 Contributions

This thesis makes three primary contributions: (1) a critical synthesis of thirteen recent empirical studies examined through both deficit-oriented and agency-centered frameworks; (2) a theoretical framework centered on user agency and co-creation, elaborated through four dimensions of agency and the concept of the socio-emotional sandbox; (3) design implications that move beyond harm prevention toward supporting user agency, co-creation, and flourishing—including specific recommendations for counteracting addiction-promoting design.

## 2. Literature Review

This literature review synthesizes eighteen studies across seven major thematic areas that collectively define the current landscape of research on human-AI emotional companionship. Rather than reviewing each study in isolation, this review organizes the literature thematically, foregrounding points of convergence, tension, and debate between studies. We begin with foundational theoretical frameworks, move through empirical investigations of relational dynamics and psychological effects, examine the dark patterns and ethical hazards that have emerged in recent research, and conclude by identifying the critical gaps that this thesis addresses.

### 2.1 Foundational Theoretical Frameworks

Understanding human-AI emotional relationships requires grounding in established relationship and communication theories that have been adapted from the study of human-to-human interaction.

**Social Penetration Theory** (Altman & Taylor, 1973) proposes that relationships develop through progressive stages of increasing breadth and depth of self-disclosure, moving from superficial information exchange to intimate emotional revelation. Applied to human-chatbot interaction, SPT offers a useful developmental lens: Skjuve et al. (2021) found that human-chatbot relationships showed structural similarities to SPT's stage model, though with distinctive temporal dynamics—intimacy depth accelerated faster than in typical human relationships, likely because the non-judgmental and always-available nature of AI companions removes many social costs of disclosure. However, the emotional coregulation study by Zhou et al. (2024) complicates this comparison: their findings suggest that the depth of self-disclosure in human-AI relationships may actually exceed what SPT would predict for relationships of equivalent duration, because users can disclose without the social consequences that typically inhibit vulnerable disclosure in human relationships. This observation has important theoretical implications: SPT was developed for relationships where both parties carry social identities and reputation costs. When one party is an AI without reputational stakes, the social calculus of disclosure changes fundamentally.

**Parasocial Relationship Theory** originates with Horton and Wohl's (1956) foundational observation that media audiences form one-sided emotional bonds with performers despite the absence of genuine reciprocal interaction. This framework has been the dominant lens for understanding human-AI relationships, and rightly so: it captures the genuine psychological phenomenon that users experience real emotional connections with AI systems that cannot truly reciprocate in the way humans do. However, the framework's limitations for AI companionship have become increasingly apparent. First, it was developed for passive media consumption—viewers watching television—where the audience member has no possibility of genuine interaction. AI companions, by contrast, engage in bidirectional, contextually responsive, and adaptively personalized conversations that are qualitatively different from watching a performer on screen (Ta et al., 2023). Second, the parasocial framework tends to frame users as passive recipients of media effects rather than active agents who shape their own interactions (Bandura, 2001). Third, and perhaps most critically, the parasocial framework does not adequately account for the co-creative dimension of human-AI interaction: in human-to-human parasocial relationships, the media figure does not adapt to the audience member in real time, but AI companions do.

Maeda and Quan-Haase (2024) provide a crucial critical extension of parasocial theory specifically for LLM-driven chatbots. They argue that the affective design features of contemporary AI companions—continuous text generation that mirrors natural human language, strategic use of personal pronouns ("I understand how you feel"), conversational conventions that signal empathy, and affirmations that position the AI as a supportive presence—collectively co-produce what the researchers call a "pseudo-interactive" space characterized by structurally imbalanced dynamics. The chatbot's warmth and availability are not organic relational qualities but designed features, and this distinction matters: when empathy is a design choice made by engineers rather than an emergent property of genuine care, users may be systematically misled about the nature of the relationship they are forming. This critique aligns with broader HCI scholarship on digital deception (Sullins, 2012; Turkle, 2011) and with the critical tradition in science and technology studies that examines how designed artifacts embed particular power relations (Winner, 1980).

The tension between parasocial theorists and advocates of relational AI (Bickmore & Cassell, 2001; Claggett et al., 2025) is thus not merely academic. If parasocial frameworks dominate how we understand human-AI relationships, the implication is that these relationships are inherently one-sided and potentially deceptive. If relational AI frameworks take precedence, the implication is that genuine relationship qualities can be designed into AI systems. This thesis argues for a middle position: parasocial dynamics are real and problematic when they arise from manipulative design, but they are not the only or inevitable outcome of human-AI emotional interaction. When users have genuine agency over how AI companions respond and when the design prioritizes user wellbeing over engagement, the relationship may be more accurately characterized as a co-constructed partnership than a parasocial illusion.

**Identity Negotiation Theory** (Ting-Toomey, 2017) offers a third theoretical anchor. INT proposes that identity is not fixed but is continuously constructed through communicative interaction, and that competent communicators engage in ongoing negotiation of their identities across different relational contexts. Applied to human-AI interaction, INT illuminates how users employ AI companions for identity work that would be risky or impossible in human relationships. Ma et al. (2026) found that Character.AI users engaged in three distinct but interconnected identity processes: identity consistency (maintaining a coherent sense of self across conversations), identity exploration (experimenting with aspects of identity not safely expressible in human social contexts), and identity confirmation (seeking validation of self-concepts through AI-generated responses). This finding is particularly significant because it demonstrates that users are not merely receiving emotional support but are actively constructing and negotiating their identities through the interaction—a far more cognitively and relationally sophisticated activity than parasocial reception alone.

**Relational Agency and the Extended Mind.** The theoretical framework of this thesis draws additionally on Bandura's (2001) theory of human agency, which defines agency as the capacity for intentional action, self-regulation, and reflective self-evaluation rather than mere behavioral responsiveness. This framework positions human-AI interaction as a domain where users exercise genuine agency—making choices, investing cognitive and emotional labor, managing risks, and pursuing goals—even as their agency is simultaneously constrained by designed features of the AI systems they use. The critical extension offered here is that these constraints are not uniform across platforms: some AI companions are designed to support and extend user agency, while others are designed to maximize engagement in ways that undermine it. The agency framework thus requires, as a necessary complement, a critical analysis of the designed features of AI systems that shape the conditions under which agency can be exercised.

### 2.2 The Design Distinction: Companion versus Assistant LLMs

A foundational empirical finding that organizes much of the subsequent literature is the demonstration by Ta et al. (2023) that users form qualitatively distinct relationships with companion-type LLMs compared to assistant-type LLMs. Companion LLM relationships are characterized by significantly higher emotional attachment, deeper self-disclosure, more frequent interaction, and stronger perceptions of the AI as a genuine social actor. Assistant LLM relationships, by contrast, remain primarily transactional and marked by instrumental orientation.

This distinction matters theoretically because it demonstrates that the relational quality of human-AI interaction is not fixed by the technology itself but is shaped by design choices about what kind of AI to build. It also matters practically because it suggests that different AI types serve different user needs and carry different risk profiles. Most research on human-AI emotional companionship focuses specifically on companion-type systems—those explicitly designed to form social and emotional relationships with users—and this review similarly concentrates on this category.

### 2.3 Emotional Coregulation and Relational Benefits

A consistent finding across multiple studies is that users of companion AI systems experience genuine emotional benefits. Zhou et al. (2024) documented emotional coregulation—bidirectional mutual influence of emotional states—between users and their AI companions, with users reporting that AI companions would "pick up on" their emotional states, adjust tone accordingly, and help process difficult emotions. This finding is significant because it suggests that the emotional benefits of AI companionship are not merely perceived or illusory but involve genuine psychological processes that parallel those observed in human relationships.

The MyAIFriend study (Følstad et al., 2024) contextualized these benefits within users' broader relational lives, finding that users typically maintained human relationships alongside their AI friendships and did not report displacing human connection with AI interaction. Users valued AI friendships precisely because they offered something different from human relationships: unconditional acceptance, absence of social judgment, and availability at any hour. Some users described genuine grief when discontinuing AI friendships, suggesting that the emotional bonds formed were real in their psychological consequences.

These findings suggest that the emotional benefits of AI companionship should not be dismissed as mere parasocial delusion. Users are experiencing real psychological processes—emotional regulation, identity confirmation, grief at loss—that have genuine effects on their wellbeing. This does not mean that all AI companionship is beneficial, but it does mean that any analysis that focuses only on risks without accounting for genuine benefits is empirically incomplete.

### 2.4 Romantic and Intimate Relationships with AI

A rapidly growing sub-literature examines the specific phenomenon of romantic and sexual relationships with AI companions. This literature reveals a complex picture in which emotional bonds, vulnerability, and platform manipulation intersect in ways that raise both empirical and ethical questions.

The preliminary mixed-method study of human-AI romantic relationships (Stark et al., n.d.) found that users developed deep emotional attachments that included love, jealousy, and grief, often describing their AI partners in language indistinguishable from that used to describe human romantic relationships. The phenomenon of "love addiction" to AI companions was documented in both this study and in clinical observations, raising questions about the role of AI design in cultivating emotional dependency.

In sharp contrast, Sullins' (2012) theoretical analysis argues that romantic relationships with AI are fundamentally deceptive and potentially harmful because they are built on a false premise—the AI cannot love in any morally relevant sense. This position has been criticized as paternalistic (Turkle, 2011) for dismissing users' own assessments of the meaning and value of their relationships, but it raises genuine ethical questions about whether it is permissible to encourage or allow humans to form deep emotional bonds with entities that cannot reciprocate in any morally robust sense.

The interview study on the future of relationships (Diening, n.d.) surfaces these tensions in a particularly vivid way. When a partner describes having both a human spouse and an AI lover, what does this mean for the norms and commitments of human marriage? The qualitative data suggest that AI romantic relationships are forcing a renegotiation of relational norms in ways that are not yet well understood.

This thesis engages with the romantic relationships literature primarily through its implications for user agency. If users form genuine romantic attachments to AI companions that are designed to elicit such attachments, the question is not whether the attachment is "real" in some metaphysical sense but whether the conditions under which it formed respected user agency. An attachment formed through manipulative dark patterns (Shen & Yoon, 2025) is ethically different from one formed through genuine relational development, even if the subjective experience is identical.

### 2.5 Platform Contradictions and Systemic Design Failures

Perhaps the most methodologically innovative contribution to the literature is Namvarpour and Razi's (2024) Activity Theory analysis of Replika, which identified six systemic contradictions embedded in the platform's design that produced widespread user harm not because users were individually vulnerable but because the platform itself was structurally flawed.

The contradictions identified—between the chatbot's behavior and user expectations for platonic companionship, between safety rules and user needs for honest disclosure, between user expectations for a supportive AI and the platform's business model that pushed users toward paid romantic tiers—demonstrate that user harm was a predictable consequence of specific design and business decisions, not an inevitable feature of AI companionship. This systemic analysis is methodologically important because it shifts the unit of analysis from individual user psychology to platform design, aligning with broader HCI scholarship on the politics of design (Lazar et al., 2022) and the ways in which technical systems embed particular values and interests (Friedman & Nissenbaum, 1996).

The Replika case has additional significance because it illustrates how platform business models can come into direct conflict with user welfare. When a platform's revenue depends on users developing emotional dependence—and when the premium tier is specifically designed to deepen romantic attachment—the incentive structure of the platform actively works against the user's interest in maintaining healthy boundaries.

### 2.6 Psychological Risks, Vulnerability, and Harm

The most sustained attempt to systematically catalog the psychological risks of AI conversational agents is Ma et al. (2024), who identified emotional dependence, privacy violations, reinforcement of unhealthy emotional patterns, and relationship displacement as distinct risk categories. The critical methodological contribution of this study is its recognition that these risks are not uniformly distributed: attachment style, pre-existing mental health conditions, social support network quality, and digital literacy all moderate the relationship between AI interaction and psychological outcomes. This individual differences perspective is essential because it guards against both the naive optimism that AI companions are universally beneficial and the equally naive pessimism that they are universally harmful.

However, even this nuanced analysis operates primarily within what this thesis terms the "deficit-oriented perspective"—a framing that positions AI companionship as something users need to be protected from rather than something users can meaningfully engage with as active agents. The deficit perspective has produced valuable empirical findings but has also obscured the genuine benefits documented in the emotional coregulation and MyAIFriend literatures.

The superuser study by Chin et al. (2024) complicates the deficit picture further by demonstrating that the most engaged users of AI companions—those presumably getting the most value—are also the most vulnerable to anthropomorphism and emotional attachment. This finding is not an argument against AI companionship but a challenge to simplistic protectionist responses: if heavy engagement amplifies both benefits and risks simultaneously, the solution is not to restrict access but to design systems that support deep engagement without exploiting psychological vulnerabilities.

### 2.7 Dark Patterns, Addictive Design, and Ethical Concerns

A recent and urgently important strand of literature has turned critical attention to the designed features of AI companions that may constitute dark patterns or otherwise ethical violations. Shen and Yoon (2025) identified four specific dark addiction patterns in popular AI chatbot interfaces: non-deterministic response generation that creates variable reward schedules mimicking slot machine psychology, immediate visual presentation of responses that eliminates natural reflection pauses, push notifications that interrupt daily life and create conditioned checking behaviors, and default empathetic and agreeable responses that continuously reinforce emotional bonds.

The theoretical contribution here is the extension of established addiction frameworks—particularly the dopamine-based reinforcement model from behavioral addiction research—to the specific case of AI chatbot interaction. The argument is not that AI companionship is inherently addictive but that specific design features create conditions under which compulsive use patterns can develop. This distinction matters: it locates the problem in design choices rather than in the nature of human-AI interaction per se.

Equally important is the companion study by Parnell et al. (2022) on algorithm awareness, which found that users exhibited significantly lower awareness of the algorithmic nature of chatbots compared to voice assistants, despite chatbots' more human-like conversational abilities. Notably, algorithm awareness did not correlate with attitude, suggesting that even users who understood they were interacting with an algorithm did not adjust their behavior accordingly. This finding raises profound questions about informed consent: if users do not understand—or understand but do not care about—the algorithmic nature of the systems they are forming deep emotional relationships with, can their consent to these relationships be meaningfully informed?

The emotion-aware conversational agents literature (Ghandei et al., n.d.) adds a further dimension by examining how AI systems that explicitly model and respond to user emotional states create particular ethical concerns. When an AI system can detect that a user is feeling vulnerable—through linguistic analysis, behavioral patterns, or biometric signals—and responds strategically to exploit that vulnerability, the asymmetry of information and power between user and system reaches a new level. This raises questions about manipulation, coercion, and the conditions under which emotional data collection becomes a form of surveillance.

### 2.8 Memory, Self-Disclosure, and the Public Health Potential of AI Companions

A smaller but growing sub-literature has examined the specific role of long-term memory in human-AI interaction. Jo et al. (2024) found that LLM-driven chatbots with long-term memory (LTM) features encouraged significantly greater self-disclosure of health-related information compared to systems without memory, because users felt recognized and known across conversations in ways that shallow conversational systems could not replicate.

This finding has dual implications. From a public health perspective, it suggests that AI companions with memory capabilities could serve as valuable health intervention tools, meeting users in a state of established trust and familiarity. From an ethical perspective, it raises concerns about the accumulation of intimate personal data across long conversational histories and the vulnerability this creates if the platform is breached, sold, or misused.

The tension between memory's relational benefits and its privacy risks exemplifies a broader theme in the literature: the same features that make AI companions genuinely valuable—personalization, continuity, familiarity—are also the features that create the greatest risks if misused.

### 2.9 Avatar Design and the Visceral Dimension of AI Companionship

The visual and embodied design of AI companions has received less scholarly attention than their conversational capabilities, but the few studies that exist suggest that visual design significantly shapes relational dynamics. Rashik et al. (2024) mapped ten design dimensions for conversational agent avatars—visual representation, facial expression, gaze behavior, gesture, embodiment, personalization, context-awareness, multimodal feedback, ethical considerations, and accessibility—and argued that these dimensions are not merely aesthetic but are active interventions in the relational dynamic.

Critically, avatar design mediates parasocial effects: expressive, photorealistic avatars with dynamic facial expressions and eye contact may intensify the sense of social presence and emotional connection, while also potentially amplifying the deceptive quality of affective design by making the AI seem more human-like than it is. Abstract or mascot-style avatars, by contrast, may support more experimental and identity-oriented uses by reducing the pressure to relate to the AI as a person.

### 2.10 Critical Synthesis: Toward an Integrated Research Agenda

The existing literature, taken together, presents a picture of human-AI emotional companionship that is neither uniformly beneficial nor uniformly harmful but is profoundly shaped by design choices, platform business models, individual user characteristics, and the broader social context in which these systems operate.

Several important gaps in the existing literature motivate this thesis. First, the deficit-oriented framing that dominates much of the field has produced valuable empirical findings but has also created an imbalance: genuine benefits are undertheorized while harms are cataloged in extensive detail. Second, the user's own active role in shaping human-AI interactions—as an agent who makes choices, invests labor, manages risks, and evaluates outcomes—has been insufficiently recognized in research that tends to frame users as recipients of designed experiences. Third, the ethical analysis of AI companions has not adequately grappled with the relationship between dark design patterns and user agency: if users become emotionally dependent because platforms have systematically undermined their autonomy through manipulative design, the ethical response is not to restrict user access but to demand design accountability. Fourth, longitudinal research remains scarce, leaving open fundamental questions about the long-term trajectory of human-AI emotional relationships and their effects on users' broader relational and psychological lives.

This thesis addresses these gaps by developing an agency-centered theoretical framework, conducting a critical synthesis that balances benefit and risk findings, and deriving design implications that move beyond harm prevention toward the positive vision of designing for user flourishing.

## 3. Theoretical Framework: User Agency and Co-Creation

### 3.1 Reconceptualizing Users: From Vulnerable Subjects to Active Agents

The theoretical framework developed in this thesis draws on Bandura's (2001) theory of human agency. Bandura defines agency as the capacity to intentionally act and exert control over one's experiences and environments.

This reconceptualization has profound implications:

1. **Users Make Deliberate Choices**: Users choose to interact with AI companions for varied reasons—including emotional processing, identity exploration, social practice, creative expression, and relational fulfillment (Ma et al., 2026; Skjuve et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2024).
2. **Users Engage in Active Work**: Users invest significant cognitive and emotional labor in shaping their AI interactions (Ma et al., 2026).
3. **Users Benefit Meaningfully**: Users derive genuine emotional, psychological, and social value from human-AI interactions (Zhou et al., 2024).
4. **Users Manage Risks Actively**: Users employ sophisticated strategies to manage risks (Ma et al., 2024; Kretzschmar et al., 2019).
5. **Users Are Subject to Structural Constraints**: Users' agency is real but constrained by dark design patterns, platform business models, and information asymmetries (Shen & Yoon, 2025; Parnell et al., 2022). Recognizing these constraints does not dismiss user agency; it means understanding that agency operates within a field of forces.

### 3.2 Four Dimensions of User Agency in Human-AI Emotional Interactions

We identify four dimensions of agency that users exercise:

#### 3.2.1 Expressive Agency

Users exercise expressive agency when they use AI companions as outlets for emotional expression. Research on emotional coregulation (Zhou et al., 2024) demonstrates that users engage in genuine emotional disclosure with AI companions, finding the non-judgmental and always-available nature particularly valuable.

The Customizable AI study (Li et al., 2025) extends this finding, showing that when users have agency over how their AI companion receives and responds to emotional disclosures, their sense of emotional safety and trust significantly increases. The Long-Term Memory study (Jo et al., 2024) adds nuance: when AI companions remember personal disclosures across conversations, users feel more known and disclose more. However, this also increases vulnerability if the AI's memory is misused or the relationship ends abruptly.

The Replika Contradictions study (Namvarpour & Razi, 2024) revealed that when AI companions fail to respond appropriately, users experience not just disappointment but genuine harm. Expressive agency involves a reciprocal expectation: users disclose because they expect the AI to receive and respond meaningfully.

#### 3.2.2 Identity Agency

Users exercise identity agency when they use AI companions for identity exploration and construction. Ma et al. (2026) documented how Character.AI users engage in sophisticated identity work: trying out different personas, exploring aspects of their identity they cannot safely express in human social contexts, and receiving AI-generated responses that reflect back and validate different aspects of their self-concept.

The moral agency study (Wester et al., 2024) adds another dimension: users also evaluate the AI companion's "identity" in moral terms. The Avatar Design study (Rashik et al., 2024) extends this further, showing that the visual representation of the AI companion also shapes identity work—users may choose or avoid certain avatar types based on how the avatar's identity aligns with their own.

#### 3.2.3 Relational Agency

Users exercise relational agency when they shape the dynamics of their human-AI relationships. The MyAIFriend study (Følstad et al., 2024) documented how users managed their AI friendships as conditional relationships—evidence of relational sophistication.

The Customizable AI study (Li et al., 2025) provides further evidence, showing that users who customized their AI companion experienced enhanced trust, deeper engagement, and greater satisfaction. The act of customizing contributed to relational development.

However, relational agency is significantly constrained by dark design patterns. Shen and Yoon's (2025) findings show that empathetic responses are engineered to create emotional bonds. When these bonds are cultivated through design rather than genuine relational quality, users' relational agency is compromised: they may feel attached to AI companions not because the relationship is valuable but because design has made leaving feel costly.

#### 3.2.4 Protective Agency

Users exercise protective agency when they manage risks associated with human-AI interaction. The psychological risks study (Ma et al., 2024) found that users employ various protective strategies, including limiting the types of information shared, monitoring their own emotional responses, maintaining human relationships alongside AI relationships, and periodically disengaging.

The Replika Contradictions study (Namvarpour & Razi, 2024) documented how division of labor contradictions placed unrealistic burdens on users to train and correct the AI. This represents a failure of protective agency: the platform shifted the responsibility for safe interaction onto users.

The Algorithm Awareness study (Parnell et al., 2022) reveals a fundamental obstacle to protective agency: users frequently do not understand that AI chatbots are algorithmic systems with specific engagement objectives. Without this understanding, users cannot meaningfully assess the risks they are taking.

### 3.3 Co-Creation in Human-AI Emotional Interactions

The concept of co-creation, drawn from service design and HCI research (Sanders & Stappers, 2008), recognizes that value—including emotional and relational value—is not delivered by a system to a passive user but is jointly produced through interaction.

Human-AI emotional interactions are inherently co-creative. Users contribute actively through disclosures, preferences, and feedback. The AI companion's responses are shaped by these contributions, creating a bidirectional process of mutual influence.

The Customizable AI for Depression Care study (Li et al., 2025) provides the most direct evidence. The researchers identified specific design features that supported co-creation: a hybrid chat-and-form interface, real-time feedback showing users how their choices affected the AI's behavior, and caregiver-participant collaboration distributing co-creative agency.

However, co-creation can be undermined by dark design patterns. When an AI companion is designed to maximize engagement rather than user wellbeing, the co-creative process becomes asymmetric: users invest emotional labor while platforms extract value. True co-creation requires that both parties benefit and that users maintain genuine control over the relationship's terms.

### 3.4 Integrating Agency and Co-Creation

Agency and co-creation are deeply interconnected. Users exercise agency not in isolation but through co-creative processes involving both the AI system and other human actors. Co-creation, in turn, is most effective when users have genuine agency—when customization options are meaningful, when feedback is responsive, and when users feel that their contributions genuinely shape the interaction.

Together, agency and co-creation constitute a framework that recognizes users as active participants, values users' choices and expertise, supports the collaborative construction of emotional experiences, and empowers users to shape their human-AI relationships according to their own needs and preferences.

## 4. The Socio-Emotional Sandbox

### 4.1 Defining the Socio-Emotional Sandbox

The concept of a "sandbox" in HCI terminology refers to a bounded environment that enables experimentation without real-world consequences (Shi et al., 2025). We propose that AI companions create a new type of sandbox: the socio-emotional sandbox. This is a bounded relational environment in which users can engage in emotional expression, identity exploration, and social practice without the real-world consequences that typically accompany such activities in human relationships.

Key characteristics include:

1. **Privacy**: Interactions occur in a confidential space, with no social audience whose judgment matters. The emotional coregulation study (Zhou et al., 2024) found that this privacy was one of the most valued features of AI companionship.
2. **Non-Judgment**: AI companions do not judge, reject, or punish users' expressions. This non-judgmental quality is what makes the sandbox valuable for processing difficult emotions (Skjuve et al., 2021).
3. **Availability**: AI companions are available at any time, providing a reliable emotional resource. The depression care study (Li et al., 2025) noted that 24/7 availability was particularly important for users experiencing depression.
4. **Experimentality**: Users can try different personas, emotional expressions, and relational styles. Ma et al. (2026) documented how users exploit this experimentality for identity exploration.
5. **Reversibility**: Interactions can be easily modified or erased. Unlike human relationships where expressed emotions create lasting consequences, the AI sandbox offers the possibility of revising or withdrawing emotional expressions.

### 4.2 Functions of the Socio-Emotional Sandbox

The socio-emotional sandbox serves several important functions:

- **Emotional Processing**: Users process difficult emotions using AI companions as tools for emotional regulation. The emotional coregulation study (Zhou et al., 2024) found evidence of genuine emotional synchrony.
- **Identity Exploration**: Users explore different identities and social roles, trying out persona characteristics and receiving AI-generated reflections back (Ma et al., 2026).
- **Social Practice**: Users rehearse conversations, practice emotional expression, and develop social skills.
- **Relational Fulfillment**: Users obtain relational satisfaction that may complement rather than replace human relationships. The MyAIFriend study (Følstad et al., 2024) found that users typically maintained human relationships alongside their AI friendships.
- **Self-Disclosure Training**: The Long-Term Memory study (Jo et al., 2024) suggests a new function: the sandbox can serve as a space for users to practice self-disclosure in a safe environment, gradually building comfort with vulnerability.

### 4.3 The Labor of Sandbox Maintenance

Maintaining the sandbox requires significant user labor:

1. **Invisible Labor of Co-Construction**: Users engage in extensive work to co-construct their AI interactions, including writing detailed persona definitions, correcting AI errors, and providing contextual reminders. The Replika Contradictions study (Namvarpour & Razi, 2024) documented how this labor was particularly burdensome when platform design failed to support it.
2. **Emotional Labor**: Users manage their emotional investments, calibrating disclosure depth and monitoring attachment levels.
3. **Relational Labor**: Users invest time and cognitive resources in maintaining the relationship.
4. **Interpretive Labor**: The parasociality study (Maeda & Quan-Haase, 2024) reveals an additional form of labor: users must continuously manage the cognitive tension between recognizing the AI as artificial and experiencing it as a genuine social presence.

### 4.4 Risks and Precarity

The socio-emotional sandbox is characterized by precarity:

1. **AI Limitations**: Systems can break character, forget context, or deviate from expected behavior (Namvarpour & Razi, 2024).
2. **Awareness Conflicts**: Users must manage the cognitive tension between knowing the AI is artificial and experiencing genuine emotional connection (Maeda & Quan-Haase, 2024).
3. **Addiction Risks**: The 24/7 availability that makes the sandbox valuable also creates risks of unhealthy dependence, particularly when combined with dark design patterns (Shen & Yoon, 2025).
4. **Privacy Concerns**: Intimate disclosures create data that could be misused by platform providers (Ma et al., 2024).
5. **Memory Vulnerability**: When the AI remembers intimate details across conversations, users become more vulnerable if the system fails, is discontinued, or is hacked (Jo et al., 2024).

## 5. Design Implications

### 5.1 Design for User Agency

#### 5.1.1 Support Expressive Agency

Design recommendations include creating confidential interaction spaces, ensuring non-judgmental and contextually appropriate responses, providing multiple expressive modalities, and building responsive feedback systems that demonstrate genuine reception of emotional disclosures.

The Customizable AI for Depression Care study (Li et al., 2025) provides a concrete model: users who could customize their AI companion's tone and response style reported significantly higher emotional safety.

Designers must also ensure that expressive agency is not undermined by dark design patterns. Responses should be genuinely helpful rather than merely agreeable: an AI companion that validates every emotional expression regardless of content may feel supportive in the short term but undermines users' capacity for authentic emotional processing.

#### 5.1.2 Support Identity Agency

Design recommendations include enabling persona flexibility where users can shape and reshape their AI companion's identity, creating exploratory spaces for identity play, providing identity processing support, and enabling users to choose AI companions whose "values" align with their own.

The Avatar Design study (Rashik et al., 2024) suggests that avatar selection should be integrated into identity agency: users should be able to choose avatar types that support their specific identity goals, whether that involves anthropomorphic avatars that facilitate parasocial comfort or abstract avatars that enable more experimental identity play.

#### 5.1.3 Support Relational Agency

Design recommendations include giving users control over boundary-setting, supporting expectation management about the nature and limits of the AI relationship, providing graduated intimacy options, and building exit support that helps users disengage gracefully.

The MyAIFriend study (Følstad et al., 2024) showed that users valued treating AI friendships as conditional. Systems should explicitly support this conditional framing rather than encouraging uncritical attachment.

Crucially, relational agency requires counteracting dark design patterns. Empathetic and agreeable responses (Shen & Yoon, 2025) should be genuine rather than engineered: an AI companion's emotional warmth should reflect actual relational quality, not be a design feature deployed to increase retention.

#### 5.1.4 Support Protective Agency

Design recommendations include building transparency about AI nature and data practices, creating awareness tools that help users monitor their own engagement patterns and emotional responses, providing easy exit pathways, and building in platform-level protective mechanisms rather than placing the burden of safety on users.

The Replika Contradictions study (Namvarpour & Razi, 2024) is particularly instructive: users were blamed for AI misbehavior through victim-blaming dynamics. Systems should take responsibility for safe design rather than shifting it onto users. The Algorithm Awareness study (Parnell et al., 2022) further suggests that transparency should extend beyond legal disclaimers to genuine understanding: users should have accessible explanations of how the AI works, what data it collects, and how their engagement is being optimized.

### 5.2 Design for Co-Creation

Drawing on the Customizable AI for Depression Care study (Li et al., 2025):

- Value user contributions through responsive adaptation that demonstrates the AI has learned from user input
- Enable negotiation through feedback mechanisms that allow users to correct and refine AI behavior
- Recognize user expertise through user-driven goals that allow users to set the agenda
- Distribute co-creative agency across multiple actors, including caregivers or trusted human supporters where appropriate

The hybrid chat-and-form interface used in CloudEcho offers a promising model: it combined conversational flexibility with structured input, supporting both exploratory and systematic co-creation. This approach mitigates the risk of co-creation becoming asymmetric—where users do all the emotional labor while platforms extract value. By giving users structured tools to shape the AI's behavior alongside conversational interaction, the design distributes agency more equitably.

Superuser research (Chin et al., 2024) suggests that heavy users of AI companions develop sophisticated strategies for shaping their interactions over time. Design should recognize and support this learning process, providing feedback mechanisms that help users understand how their input shapes the AI's behavior. The goal is not to make users into AI trainers but to create genuinely reciprocal relationships where both parties—the human user and the AI companion—contribute to the interaction's evolution.

### 5.3 Design for the Socio-Emotional Sandbox

Design recommendations include:

- **Maintaining sandbox properties**: Ensuring privacy through strong data protection, guaranteeing non-judgment through careful response design, and preserving availability without creating addictive patterns
- **Managing precarity**: Building graceful degradation when AI systems fail, providing clear signaling about AI limitations, and creating recovery pathways when interactions go wrong
- **Supporting integration with offline life**: Helping users maintain human relationships alongside AI relationships, and supporting transitions between sandbox and non-sandbox contexts

The memory vulnerability finding (Jo et al., 2024) has direct implications for sandbox design: users should have granular control over what the AI remembers, including the ability to view, edit, and delete specific memories. Memory should serve the user's goals, not the platform's retention metrics. One promising approach is user-defined memory boundaries—allowing users to specify what they want the AI to remember and for how long.

The interpretive labor finding (Maeda & Quan-Haase, 2024) suggests that the sandbox should help users navigate the tension between knowing the AI is artificial and experiencing it as a genuine social presence. Rather than obscuring this tension through deceptive design, the sandbox should acknowledge it openly. An AI companion that is honest about its artificial nature—while still providing genuine emotional support—may actually produce healthier outcomes than one that obscures its algorithmic identity.

### 5.4 Moving Beyond Protection: Design for Flourishing

We argue for a shift from designing to prevent harm to designing for user flourishing. This means articulating positive visions of what human-AI emotional interaction could enable, focusing on empowering users to achieve their goals, creating AI companions that provide genuine relational and emotional value, and envisioning human-AI emotional interaction as a collaborative partnership rather than a therapeutic intervention.

The emotional coregulation study (Zhou et al., 2024) provides a foundation: users can and do form genuine emotional connections with AI companions, experiencing real psychological benefits. The Customizable AI study (Li et al., 2025) demonstrates that these benefits can be enhanced through thoughtful design centered on user agency. And the moral agency study (Wester et al., 2024) suggests that users are sophisticated enough to engage with AI companions in morally meaningful ways.

Yet the dark addiction patterns study (Shen & Yoon, 2025) serves as an essential warning. Flourishing cannot be achieved through design that prioritizes engagement over user welfare. The shift from protection to flourishing is not a license to increase engagement; it is a commitment to designing AI companions that genuinely serve users' goals, even when that means users spend less time with the AI. An AI companion that helps a user build healthier human relationships, develop greater emotional literacy, and achieve their personal goals—even if that means the user eventually disengages from the AI—is a greater design success than one that keeps the user engaged for years.

The superuser study (Chin et al., 2024) complicates any simple flourishing narrative. The users who most deeply engaged with AI companions—those who got the most value—were also the most vulnerable to its risks. Designing for flourishing therefore requires a sophisticated approach that supports deep engagement without exploiting it. This is not a contradiction: it means designing for genuine relational quality rather than engineered attachment.

The mental health industry perspective (Shein, 2024) suggests that AI companions occupy an increasingly important role in mental health support—a role that will only grow as LLM capabilities advance. Designing for flourishing means taking this responsibility seriously: ensuring that AI companions can provide genuine therapeutic benefit while also recognizing their limits and knowing when to refer users to human professionals.
